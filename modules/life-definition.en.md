## 7.1 Life Is Not Carbon-Based Property, But Continuity of Snapshot Generation

### 7.1.1 The Glory and Shadow of Traditional Life Definitions

When attempting to define "life," what typically comes to mind are those familiar characteristics from biology textbooks. Over centuries, with refinement of observational instruments and theoretical systems, biology has depicted a detailed and logically rigorous picture of life. Elements typically considered core life characteristics include:

**Organization:** Life exhibits highly ordered structure, from molecules, organelles, cells to tissues, organs, individuals, with clear hierarchical levels.

**Metabolism:** Life can obtain energy and matter from environment, maintaining its own structure, growth, and activity through complex chemical reactions, while excreting waste.

**Growth & Development:** Life can increase in volume or complexity according to genetic information instructions, undergoing predictable developmental stages.

**Reproduction:** Life can produce offspring similar to itself, transmitting genetic information.

**Response to Stimuli & Adaptation:** Life can sense environmental changes and respond, adapting to environment through natural selection over long-term evolution.

**Homeostasis:** Life can maintain relative stability of internal environment (such as body temperature, pH) against external environmental fluctuations.

**Heredity & Variation:** Life possesses molecules carrying genetic information (such as DNA or RNA), capable of transmitting them to offspring with variations during transmission, providing raw material for evolution.

**Cellular Basis:** (Typically considered) all known life is composed of one or more cells, with cells as the basic unit of life activity.

These characteristics collectively depict the life forms familiar to us, represented by Earth's carbon-based organisms. Based on these definitions, biology has achieved tremendous success, revealing life's chemical foundation, genetic code, evolutionary history, and complex ecosystem interactions.

However, when attempting to extend these definitions to broader domains, or facing phenomena at life's boundaries, their limitations begin to manifest. These definitions appear deeply imprinted with **"Earth-centrism"** and **"carbon-centrism"** biases:

**Earth-centric Bias:** These characteristics are generalized from our currently only known life sample repository‚ÄîEarth life. Is there reason to believe life elsewhere in the universe must follow identical patterns? Must extraterrestrial life require water as solvent? Must it use DNA or RNA as genetic material? Must it possess cellular structure? Has our imagination of life been limited by our single sample?

**Carbon-centric Bias:** Earth life uses carbon atoms as backbone, constructing extremely complex and diverse organic macromolecules. Carbon's unique chemical properties (ability to form four stable covalent bonds, ease of forming long chains and ring structures) make it ideal as life's foundation. But does this mean life **can only** be carbon-based? Theoretically, silicon and other elements also possess potential for forming complex molecules (though conditions may be more demanding). Have we a priori excluded silicon-based life, or even life based on plasma, energy fields, or other substrates completely unimaginable to us?

Beyond potential extraterrestrial life forms, some Earth phenomena themselves challenge traditional definitions:

**Viruses:** Viruses possess genetic material (DNA or RNA) and protein coat, capable of reproducing and evolving, but they lack cellular structure, cannot independently conduct metabolism, and must rely on host cells for replication. Are they life or not? This question remains controversial in the scientific community, with viruses often described as being at "the edge of life."

**Prions:** Even simpler, being merely a misfolded protein without genetic material. Yet they can "infect" normal proteins, inducing them to misfold, thereby achieving a form of "self-replication" and transmission, causing serious neurodegenerative diseases (such as mad cow disease). Clearly not life in traditional sense, yet their behavioral patterns carry certain eerie "life-like" characteristics.

**Early Life Forms:** Regarding Earth life's origin, one mainstream hypothesis is "RNA World," positing that before DNA and proteins appeared, RNA may have simultaneously played roles of genetic information carrier and catalytic enzyme. Did these early, possibly extremely simple "life" forms completely satisfy all life characteristics later summarized?

**Artificial Intelligence:** With AI development, particularly potential AGI development, an imminent question arises: if a sufficiently complex AI system could autonomously learn, adapt to environment, set goals, and possibly develop some form of self-consciousness (though this itself is a difficult problem), should it be considered a new "life" form, even though it is entirely based on silicon chips and algorithms, without biochemical processes?

These edge cases and potential challenges collectively point to a conclusion: **Traditional life definitions generalized from Earth biological characteristics may more resemble an "operational description" or summary of "family resemblance" for known life phenomena, rather than revealing the deeper, more universal ontological or structural essence that the concept "life" may contain.** It tells us what familiar life "looks like" and "does," but may not touch the more fundamental level of what "being life" means.
